Avatar: The Last Airbender: 8 / 10

Book 1: 6 / 10 I was bored, browsed Netflix, saw this, figured I’d see what all the hype was about. I’d say this first arc was mostly just setup. Don’t get me wrong, the characters, world-building, and general atmosphere are great. But nothing much of substance happens barring the first few and last few episodes. In between, we get episodic mini-adventures that tell childish lessons, and that’s about it. Yeah yeah yeah, it’s a children’s show with 12 year old main characters so I’ll cut it some slack.

General impressions: Katara’s my favorite character so far. Aang is a kiddie and Sokka is comedic relief. Zuko’s a bit too jaded for my tastes. Looking forward to his character development.

Book 2: 8 / 10 I really enjoyed this season. I love Toph (the blind gimmick especially) and Azula (something about being confidently yet charismatically evil has a special place in my heart) as characters, and the season did a really good job of tying old threads back into the story. Iroh continues to be MVP, and the plot is progressing at a good pace. The additional bending variety is also pretty neat. Zuko’s a real mess though. I find it real hard to see how he’s gonna redeem himself as a character.

Book 3: 9 / 10 Okay, so almost a month later, I still haven’t watched the final episode. Nonetheless, season three is definitely the highlight of the series. As Sozin’s Comet approaches, we reach a point in the story in which there’s very little filler. Character development has occurred, meaning cooler fights and less immaturity. We get throwbacks to previous episodes (welcome back, Suki), and in general, the show does a great job heading towards its climax.

Update: Sozin’s Comet was pretty good.

Attack on Titan Season 1: 7 / 10

It’s funny how opinions change. When I first watched AoT years ago, I never continued past the first season because I thought the characters were just way too bland. This time around, I still found Eren extremely annoying (Armin too, but slightly less so), but I didn’t have a problem with the rest of them. In particular, Mikasa gets a pass on her clingy behavior and blatant op-ness, since her tragic backstory was actually pretty good. But in terms of the supporting cast, I enjoyed watching Jean change, and the rest of them don’t get enough screen time to really matter.

The plot, action, and production quality are more than enough to make AoT worth watching, despite Eren being a whiny dumb-ass (Jeez I hate this guy). The premise is genius, and the show does a good job showcasing tragedy and death without being too in your face about it. Maybe it’s because I’ve grown up a bit (and I guess I’ve watched more stuff), but I really appreciate seeing the bleak, hopeless side of an adrenaline-pumping action show.

Music is hype, animation is stunning (holy cow, this was made in 2013?), and I’m super excited to binge the rest of the series.

Finished: 2/7/2021

Attack on Titan Season 2: 5 / 10

Well, the production quality is still there, and the fights continue to be cool. I want to highlight the music. Opening song is inspiring, battle music is hype, and I have a morbid fascination with the ending song. But I gotta say, the content of this season was pretty questionable. There were so many plot twists, that I have absolutely no idea what’s gonna happen. Not like Game of Thrones where it keeps you on your feet, more like “alright what kind of bullshit will they come up with this time.” It also seems like this show has a reverse plot armor, where the bad guys never really lose. One final complaint - way too many flashbacks and tangential story lines. I really don’t care about Sasha or Ymir’s backstory.

That being said, the scene where Reiner just broke down and said “Yo Eren, by the way, I’m the armored titan…” was pretty hilarious. Reiner and Berholdt as complex villains filled with regrets was done very well. Also want to point out that Erwin really showed his colors as a total bad-ass during that rescue sequence.

Ultimately, I think this season wasn’t very good if I step back and try to look at it objectively. I think the season was trying to do too much. But at least we’re starting to get plot progression. Things are happening, mysteries are being uncovered, and we’re learning that there’s some sort of crazy conspiracy going on. The hype train don’t stop, onto S3.

Tenet: 7 / 10

That really is the theme of the movie. Tenet was a confusing mess. Beautiful and well-crafted, but a confusing mess nonetheless. I had absolutely no idea what was happening during my first watch-through. After reading some online resources (very well made diagram shown below), I re-watched the movie two weeks later. This time around, I actually understood what was happening. Nonetheless, a good movie shouldn’t require Tenet’s level of mental gymnastics, so I can’t rate it that highly. I do have an appreciation for Nolan’s misguided genius though.

I won’t even attempt to tackle Tenet’s plot holes. The entire premise is based on time paradoxes, so like the scientist lady said, I won’t try to understand it. There are definitely inconsistencies in the story though.

Sound mixing was the worst I’ve ever heard, no exaggeration. I’m so glad I watched this at home with subtitles rather than in a theater.

Characters were very weak. I hated the whole Kat character arc (“Everyone will die. ” “Including my son?!”), and the protagonist’s obsession with her. There wasn’t any real emotion or character depth/complexity.

All in all, a cool movie but with an overly-ambitious premise. Shoutouts to the final battle scene, a visual cacaphony of high budget action. I especially appreciated the scene where they blew up a building in forward and inverse at the same time.

Attack on Titan Final Season Part 1: 4 / 10

I was not a fan of this season. I have to start off by talking about the CGI. My God, my eyes were bleeding during episode 6. I was pretty excited up until that point, and then the CGI just killed all my hype. I get that there was a time crunch to produce this season, but CGI’ing human characters? It’s so noticeable, especially after AoT put together some of the best animation ever seen in the previous season. It didn’t help how different the characters looked with the new studio + 4 year time skip.

Speaking of characters, I’m very disappointed with Eren’s character writing. At this point, he’s a completely different character. There was no buildup to this change, simply a 4 year time skip and a completely new character, throwing out everything from the previous 3 seasons. The table scene where Eren lays it down on Armin and Mikasa was inexcusable. Nothing he does from this point on can redeem his character. That’s not even mentioning the hypocrisy of his genocidal actions and all the people dying because of him - friend and foe. The 180 in characterization is so extreme that I’d consider it bad writing. Even if there’s some excuse further down the line (mind control, double agent, etc), I don’t see how my opinion will change.

I respect the AoT’s writing and how it takes time to follow the “enemy” side. The moral ambiguity, world-building, and plot progression are top notch as expected. But the downgrade in production value, change in characterization, and fact that this show is completely different from what I originally signed up for ruined the experience for me.

Also, I hate Gabi’s character. Yeah, sure she’s a brainwashed kid. Doesn’t change the fact that she’s annoying. Constantly uses the fact that she’s a little kid to commit war crimes and kill empathetic enemies. Not to mention that she shouldn’t have the capabilities to kill the people she killed. Imagine surviving years of grusome war just to get shot by a little kid.

Minari: 8 / 10

Minari is a nice slice of life film that I found to be very relatable. While the main cast was a Korean family moving to rural Arkansas (rather than a Chinese family in Minnesota), a lot of Jacob’s mannerisms were very similar to my dad’s. Right from the get-go, we’re presented with a lower-class immigrant father with dreams of making it big in the US. While I won’t point out all of the similarities (Some of them hit a bit too close to home), I will say that the beginning scene where Jacob asks his family to sleep together on the floor is exactly the type of strange joke (is it a joke?) that my dad would tell.

While I haven’t lived in the country, the whole movie felt very real. Not in a No Country for Old Men way where the dialogue is simple and realistic rather than script-like and entertaining. But in a relatable “these character interactions are exactly what I’ve seen in real life” sort of way. The way Anne helped out and did things with her little brother at times while expressing annoyance at others was spot on. So was the casual racism that everyone in the film just accepted/ignored and didn’t make a big deal of. And the dichotomy of Jacob/Monica’s relationship. Again, the keyword for this movie is “relatable.”

Close to the ground and wide camera shots made the movie feel more personal, as if you were physically there and taking in the scenery. A low-key musical score helped with this and was very fitting.

I do wish the movie did a bit more to tie together some of the earlier scenes. The minari motif felt very weak (and forced into the ending), and I thought there would be more to the snake in the woods, Mountain Dew, and paper airplanes, among other things.

Overall, a very pleasing movie. I’ll recommend it to my family.

Seaspiracy: 4 / 10

I appreciate that this “documentary” brings important points to attention. Overfishing IS a huge problem. But I get the same vibes as The Social Dilemma. There’s a clear animal rights agenda at play, and I can’t help feeling that the narrator is misrepresenting the situation to look a lot worse than it actually is. Extreme claims and life-or-death thriller chases get the views, but how accurate are they? Pirate vigilantes are cool and all, but is that the solution we should be endorsing? The “documentary” suggests not eating any fish as the only solution, and that’s completely un-realistic and a one-dimensional take on a complex issue. I also disliked Ali’s passive aggressive/confrontational attitude. Like, if you forge a business card and sneak into a fishing conference, what do you think people will say when you ask them if they employ slave labor? Really?

So I guess props for the execution and bringing the issue to light. But a lot of issues with the actual content.

Joker: 7 / 10

Big props to Joaquin Phoenix for absolutely nailing the character. Arthur Fleck’s character was unnerving yet sympathy inducing. Pitiful at times, deplorable at others. The film really does make you stop and think about those who are dealt a bad hand. We see Arthur make an earnest attempt to dig himself out of his hole, to no avail.

Staircase symbolism was pretty neat, small victories occurring as he climbed up stairs and major defeats as he descended into madness. Also liked the parallels at the beginning and end: chasing after some kids and later running from the cops. In a way, it paints a picture of initial hope (chasing after a goal), eventually dashed (running away from his problems) as he gives up and embraces his anger towards unfair world.

I also appreciate the shaky camera work, giving the whole film a fittingly un-refined look. In general, everything in the film was fitting for Arthur’s situation. The run-down apartment, garbage everywhere, and a drab color palette in general. I don’t recall anything special about the sound production, which probably means it was good.

While I do appreciate the film, I can’t say that I particularly liked it. It’s not like Whiplash where I hated watching it but walked away saying “damn, that was amazing.” Yeah, some of the acting WAS amazing, but the whole vibe of the show and how it concluded doesn’t really sit right with me. Maybe it’s because I’ve been pretty lucky all my life, never experiencing pain to Arthur’s extent. But surely the message here isn’t to just give up and start murdering people. Really, I feel like the film didn’t have a message. It was just a depressing showcase of an unlucky man. So in that way, it’s not particularly memorable or enlightening.

The Father: 7 / 10

Anthony Hopkins put on a great performance, worthy of his Oscar. I personally appreciated Olivia Colman’s acting a bit more though. Her subtle sadness and despair was touching.

The Father was, for the most part, what I expected going in. A sad story detailing the downward spiral of an old man with dementia. It was heartbreaking, especially at the end when Hopkins just completely broke down like a baby. However, the movie didn’t quite do it for me emotionally. I think it’s because it was primarily from Anthony’s POV. For the most part, it just felt confusing and deja-vu-ey to the audience, which is probably what it’s really like to have dementia. But ignorance is bliss, and I feel like dementia’s more painful for everyone else. Anne’s helpless sadness was so agonizing to watch. On one hand, I wish we could’ve seen more of her perspective. On the other, I get that the movie would be drastically worse if they tried it - we wouldn’t get Anthony’s inside look to really empathize with him.

Set design was spectacular. I love all the little touches they put in to differentiate scenes and to showcase the dementia at work. Pausing for a few minutes and comparing the side-to-side of the different-but-similar kitchen shots was fun. Also, just wanted to say, damn the people in this movie were well-dressed.

Memories of Murder: 4 / 10

Man, writing this a day later, I still don’t know how to feel about it. Objectively, it was pretty good. Good storytelling, directing, and Bong Joon Ho’s classic (but well-appreciated) in-your-face presentation of themes. But the ending was just so dissatisfying… I get it. That’s the point. Again, I want to commend the movie. It’s objectively pretty good, it just didn’t hit for me. And that’s okay.

Violet Evergarden: 7 / 10

First and foremost, Violet Evergarden is the most gorgeous anime I’ve seen. It doesn’t blow a money gun in your face the way Promare or Fate does. Rather, it’s a subtle and consistent beauty. Pause the stream anytime and you have something screensaver worthy. The classical music and rich color scheme added to the refinement.

My next piece of major praise is some of the single-episode vignettes as Violet visits various people. While we don’t have time to build a real relationship with these side characters, the complexity of the relationships and nuance of the emotions was brought out in a way I’ve rarely seen before. The episode with the child/dying mother was beautifully done and really captured the confusion, conflict, and sadness you might expect in such a situation. Brought some tears to my eyes at the end, which rarely happens.

So given the animation quality and emotions some of the episodes brought forth, I’ve gotta give the show some major props. That being said, I hated a lot of other aspects of the show. Violet Evergarden is a dull character. Her character growth is one-dimensional and flat. Her backstory doesn’t actually make me feel sad, and I got annoyed at how much it got brought up. Yeah I get it, you had a traumatic past and you’re emotionally stunted. I don’t want to be told how to feel. I don’t need flashbacks to the Major every 5 minutes.

I also felt that the non-vignette episodes fell flat. Like I said before, Violet’s struggle with her past progresses in a boring and expected way. The ending also felt hugely contrived. Physics-defying action sequences, utterly stupid decision-making (wtf Violet, you’re literally getting shot at, do something), and laughable plot-armor (Hey, we have these bayonets, let’s try to stab her instead of shooting. And if we do shoot at point-blank, let’s miss). Ruined my immersion and left a bad taste in my mouth to end the show.

Violet Evergarden has its ups and downs. I’d say it’s rather average most of the way through, but when it does elevate, it brings it to 100%.

Dune: 7 / 10

Book 1 was finished back in 2020. I loved it and gave it 9 / 10.

Book 2: 9 / 10 Book 2 was probably my favorite third of the novel. Herbert’s world-building is truly wonderful. The amount of detail put into the world of Arrakis is insane, not to mention all the political machinations, Fremen cultural/linguistic intricacies, and lore. It’s clearly a made-up fantasy world, yet everything, from the character interactions to the technology to the ecosystems, just feels so sensical.

The main characters aren’t particularly interesting in-of-themselves (Paul and Jessica are OP/unrelatable, Vladimir/Feyd-Reutha are caricatures of evil, etc). However, the story-telling more than makes up for it. There’s so much plot setup; it’s clear some major shit is gonna go down. I guess the premonitions of the future also indicate that pretty clearly.

Book 2 Finished: 5/7/2021

Book 3: 4 / 10 Book 3 was rather disappointing. The first problem was pace. While the first book took it’s time setting the stage, and the second book building Arrakis, the third book rushed to get to Muad’dib’s assured victory against the world.

Maybe I could’ve seen this coming, but my other problem was the plot progression. There’s so much destiny happening that it’s rather clear how the story will progress. At this point, reading was somewhat of a formality to get to the end. Herbert’s writing style remained captivating, especially during tense moments like the Feyd-Rautha kanly. However, the actual plot points and dialogue weren’t particularly moving, which made for a boring end to an otherwise great novel.

The Vegetarian: 5 / 10

The Vegetarian was one of those slow motion train wrecks that you can’t look away from. I’m not even sure if I liked the book, but at the very least it was interesting and a pretty quick read.

I think the book’s main goal is to act as a character study for internal mental struggles and the world’s general lack of empathy and communication. Me included. All three chapters are tied together by Yeong-hye, the main character of sorts, who slowly devolves from a quirky, yet functional member of society into a psych ward patient who refuses to eat anything because she thinks she’s a tree.

The first chapter, which is from the perspective of her husband, sets the scene and is summarized by a lack of support for Yeong-hye. Rather, her husband and family are engrossed with the idea that she must eat meat, and that something is wrong with her. I’d be frustrated too if I were her husband. But her father’s force-feeding, and everyone’s lack of empathy were very sad to read. To be honest, I don’t entirely blame them though. Something really is wrong with Yeong-hye, and she acts in an entirely uncooperative way, refusing to elaborate or explain why, like a normal adult. It’s fair to have your own reasons for something, but when everyone around you is trying to “help” and you don’t offer anything other than “I had a dream,” it can be very frustrating to work with. Then again, we don’t really know what’s going on in Yeong-hye’s mind, and I guess that’s part of the point.

The next chapter was from the perspective of her sister In-hye’s husband. A coddled artist with a screw or two loose in his head. I was not a fan of this section. It was weird, but most of all, In-hye’s husband just seemed like an asshole. We got to see his thought-process and his obsession first-hand, but I guess for me at least, his actions were predatory, wrong, and irredeemable. I think more than his actions, I was saddened by his lack of care for his family.

Finally, the last chapter focused on In-hye - a single-mother business owner who has to take on the burden of Yeong-hye’s care. Leading up to this section, I felt bad for her, and even moreso reading it. Whereas she’s sort of the perfect person on the outside in the previous sections, we find out that her sense of duty stems from childhood trauma and that she’s barely holding everything together. Once again, it really shows you that people are going through shit that you don’t realize. Her life just seems miserable, especially having to deal with her sister, who’s a stick (mentally and physically) at this point.

Book ends without much of a conclusion other than feels bad. Make up my own inferences and whatnot. Sad.
